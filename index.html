How the predictions work

Every probability on this page comes from one transparent model rather than gut feel:

  1. Team strength — Elo ratings. Each of the 48 teams carries a World Football Elo rating (eloratings.net snapshot, 11 June 2026; pre-tournament top values confirmed against press reports — Spain 2157, Argentina 2115, France 2063, England 2024, Brazil 1991 — with remaining values closely estimated from the published early-2026 tables). These ratings then move after every result (see "Live recalibration" below). Elo is the best single predictor of international results because it weights opponent strength, margin and match importance.
  2. Host advantage. Mexico, the USA and Canada receive a +50 Elo boost when playing inside their own country — roughly the historically observed World Cup host effect (hosts outperform their ranking in ~80% of tournaments).
  3. Win probability. The classic Elo formula P = 1 / (1 + 10^(−diff/400)) converts the rating gap into a head-to-head expectation.
  4. Scorelines — a Dixon-Coles grid. Expected goals for each side are derived from the rating gap around a 1.4-goal baseline, then a joint scoreline grid gives the probability of every result. A plain Poisson grid would multiply the two teams' goal counts as if they were independent, which is known to under-state 0-0 and 1-1 and over-state 1-0/0-1; we apply the standard Dixon-Coles correction (a single low-score correlation parameter, ρ = −0.114) that re-weights exactly those four corner cells to match how real matches behave. The projected exact score is the most likely cell of this grid, constrained to agree with the predicted outcome.
  5. Draws. The draw probability is simply the diagonal of that same Dixon-Coles grid, so the scoreline and the 1X2 numbers can never contradict each other. The correlation parameter is calibrated so evenly matched sides draw about 28% (the historical World Cup group-stage rate), and the chance decays realistically to single digits for lopsided ties — no separate draw curve or fudge multiplier. The Elo expectation then sets the win/loss split of the remaining probability. (Two earlier versions — a standalone Gaussian draw curve, then a global draw-rate multiplier on an independent grid — have both been retired in favour of this single calibrated correlation.)
  6. Groups & knockouts. Group tables rank teams by expected points across all six fixtures (already-played results count at face value). The knockout tree advances the higher-probability side in every tie; in knockouts the draw mass is reallocated to the stronger team, which would also be favoured across extra time and penalties.
  7. Tournament probabilities. The Odds & value tab runs a 10,000-iteration Monte Carlo simulation: every group game is sampled from its W/D/L probabilities, tables are resolved (points → sampled goal swing → Elo), the Round-of-32 template is filled, and the knockout tree is played out with sampled winners. Champion/finalist/semi-final probabilities are the run frequencies.
  8. Live recalibration. After each match the two teams' Elo ratings are updated with the standard World Football Elo formula — R_new = R_old + K·G·(result − expected), K = 60 for World Cup matches, with a goal-difference multiplier G (1.5 for a two-goal win, larger for bigger margins). Played results also count at face value in the group tables. So every projection on the page reflects what has actually happened so far, not just the pre-tournament snapshot.

Context the model also leans on

The written rationale for each match blends the numbers with scouting context: FIFA ranking (April 2026), recent form, squad notes, star players, and tournament history (e.g. Morocco's 2022 semi-final run, Japan's wins over Germany and Spain, England's perfect qualifying campaign, Mexico's unbeaten record in home World Cup openers).

Honest limitations

  • Elo snapshots can't see late injuries, suspensions or squad rotation on dead-rubber matchday threes.
  • Single-match football is wildly random: a 65% favourite still loses or draws more than one game in three. Read the probabilities, not just the picks.
  • The knockout bracket shown is one illustrative path through FIFA's 495 possible Round-of-32 layouts.
  • Mid-tier Elo values are close estimates where a live June 2026 figure wasn't independently confirmable.
