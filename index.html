How the predictions work

Every probability on this page comes from one transparent model rather than gut feel:

  1. Team strength — Elo ratings. Each of the 48 teams carries a World Football Elo rating (eloratings.net snapshot, 11 June 2026; top teams confirmed against press reports — Spain 2157, Argentina 2115, France 2063, England 2024, Brazil 1991 — with remaining values closely estimated from the published early-2026 tables). Elo is the best single predictor of international results because it weights opponent strength, margin and match importance.
  2. Host advantage. Mexico, the USA and Canada receive a +50 Elo boost when playing inside their own country — roughly the historically observed World Cup host effect (hosts outperform their ranking in ~80% of tournaments).
  3. Win probability. The classic Elo formula P = 1 / (1 + 10^(−diff/400)) converts the rating gap into a head-to-head expectation.
  4. Draws. Group games get a draw probability of up to 28% for evenly matched sides, decaying with the rating gap (Gaussian, width 650) — calibrated to the ~25–30% historical draw rate in World Cup group stages.
  5. Scorelines. Expected goals for each side are derived from the rating gap around a 1.4-goal baseline, and the most likely exact score is read off a joint Poisson grid, constrained to agree with the predicted outcome.
  6. Groups & knockouts. Group tables rank teams by expected points across all six fixtures (already-played results count at face value). The knockout tree advances the higher-probability side in every tie; in knockouts the draw mass is reallocated to the stronger team, which would also be favoured across extra time and penalties.

Context the model also leans on

The written rationale for each match blends the numbers with scouting context: FIFA ranking (April 2026), recent form, squad notes, star players, and tournament history (e.g. Morocco's 2022 semi-final run, Japan's wins over Germany and Spain, England's perfect qualifying campaign, Mexico's unbeaten record in home World Cup openers).

Honest limitations

  • Elo snapshots can't see late injuries, suspensions or squad rotation on dead-rubber matchday threes.
  • Single-match football is wildly random: a 65% favourite still loses or draws more than one game in three. Read the probabilities, not just the picks.
  • The knockout bracket shown is one illustrative path through FIFA's 495 possible Round-of-32 layouts.
  • Mid-tier Elo values are close estimates where a live June 2026 figure wasn't independently confirmable.
